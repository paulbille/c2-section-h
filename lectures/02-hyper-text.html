HyperText?

note:

  • let’s talk about hyper-text
  • How would you define it?

Hypertext is text displayed on a computer display or other electronic devices with references (hyperlinks) to other text that the reader can immediately access.

Wikipedia, Hypertext

note:

  • a narrow definition of hypertext

note:

All texts, in a sense, are hypertext. You come to a word you don't understand, so you look it up in the dictionary. You read a passage and you stop and you think about another book; you may even put it down and go get another book off your bookshelf and read something about something else. Texts are not linear. Texts are multiple and for anybody who really reads and enjoys reading, it is an interactive process.

Samuel Delany, Samuel Delany and Octavia Butler, MIT 1998

Nelson's Interwingularity

Proto HyperText
...

Paul Otlet, Cellula Mundáneum

note:

  • In 1934 Belgian bibliographer, Paul Otlet proposed a proto-hypertext system in which all documents would be decomposed down to unique phrases stored on interlinked index cards forming a network of electric telescopes.

note:

  • Together with Henri La Fontaine he founded the Mundaneum in Mons, Belgium
  • an institution meant to gather together all the world's knowledge.

Vaneevar Bush's Memex

note:

  • There is of course, Jorge Luis Borges' book The Garden of Forking Path's, often cited as the earliest inspiration for the concept of hypertext.
  • But most famously perhaps, there is Vaneevar Bush's 1945 essay As We May Think and the Memex, his idea of a mechanized information retrieval system mimicking the associative, trailing nature of our minds.

Ted Neslon's Xanadu

note:

  • Even though HTTP is standard today, the web is just one implementation of a hypertext system.
  • Ted Nelson, our interwingled guy from the Computer Lib/Dreammachines zine imagined the web to be bi-directional instead of one-directional.
  • mention transclusion: “The result of transclusion is a single integrated document made of parts assembled dynamically from separate sources, possibly stored on different computers in disparate places.”
  • Ted Nelson's Project Xanadu was a hypertext system that he began conceptualizing in the 1960s.
  • Unlike the one-way links we have on the web, Xanadu's bi-directional links enabled users to not only reference information but also maintain direct connections to the sources they cited.

1962, Douglas Engelbart, NLS (oN-Line System)

note:

  • In 1962 at Stanford Research Institute, computer pioneer Douglas Engelbart started working on the NLS, or the "oN-Line System".
  • When Engelbart demoed it in 1968, today known as The Mother of All Demos, it wasn't only the first time that the practical use of hypertext links was demonstrated
  • but also many of the fundamental elements of modern personal computing like windows, video conferencing, the computer mouse, word processing, dynamic file linking, revision control, and a collaborative real-time editor.

HES, Brown University 1967

note:

  • The Hypertext Editing System (HES) was an early hypertext research project conducted at Brown University in 1967 by Andries van Dam, Ted Nelson, and several Brown students.
  • It was the first hypertext system available on commercial equipment that novices could use.

"A second brain"

note:

  • especially in note-taking the idea of linking your disparate notes into hyper-linked structures has found a lot of believers in recent years

note:

  • you will find a whole niche of productivity fanatics that swear on the idea of linking
  • it's a bit of a cult

  • the idea being that our brain neurons are also a network of nodes that have a similar structure
  • so the argument being that if you use organize your notes on your computer like neurons in your brain, you will be more productive, smarter and over all a better human

Niklas Luhmann's literal Zettelkasten

note:

  • a lot of the idea's around this niche are based on Niklas Luhmann's Zettelkasten method
  • He was a german sociologist and prominent thinker in systems theory
  • A Zettelkasten (German for "slip box", plural Zettelkästen) or card file consists of small items of information stored on paper slips or cards that may be linked to each other through subject headings ==or other ==metadata such as numbers and tags

note:

  • obviously not everything works for everyone
  • so you need to figure out yourself what is the best way for your to make sense of your thoughts
  • But still: Linking practices—referencing other material—are at the core of any contemporary approach to design and more often than not, our job as designers can be broken down to connecting the dots.
