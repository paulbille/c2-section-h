Free Information Part 0: Information wants to be free...
...

note:

  • In 1966, a young man named Stewart Brand handed out buttons in San Francisco reading: “Why haven't we seen a photograph of the whole Earth yet?”

Earthrise, by William Anders, Apollo 8, 1968

note:

  • Then in 1968 the NASA pubslished this photo titled Earthrise as seen from Apollo 8 spacecraft while orbiting the Moon

note:

  • Two years later, the NASA photograph of the blue marble appeared on the cover of the Whole Earth Catalog.
  • Some of you might remember this from our first lecture.
  • Again, Whole Earth is often described as the analogue forerunner of Google, and it’s often considered one of the most influential publications within the last couple of decades
  • It mediated between cyberneticists and hippies, nature romantics and technologists, psychedelia and computer culture, and triggered some defining impulses for the environmentalist movement and the rise of the digital network culture.

  • This is brand and his team working on the catalog in 1971.

The ARC team rehearses for the demo at SRI headquarters in Menlo Park, 1968.

note:

  • Steward Brand started out as a photographer, but is most known for his work as publisher of the Whole Earth Catalog, and on the most general level he is propably best described as a technologist.
  • Brand believes that with technology, we can reshape the world that we made for ourselves into something environmentally and socially sustainable.

Information wants to be free!

note:

  • Brand is often quoted for saying this.
  • What do you all think?
  • Does information want to be free?

On the one hand you have—the point you’re making Woz—is that information sort of wants to be expensive because it is so valuable—the right information in the right place just changes your life. On the other hand, information almost wants to be free because the costs of getting it out is getting lower and lower all of the time. So you have these two things fighting against each other.

Stewart Brand, in conversation with Steve Wozniack at the Hackers Conference in 1984

Information wants to be free. Information also wants to be expensive. ...That tension will not go away.

Stewart Brand, The Media Lab: Inventing the Future at MIT

note:

  • This is the full quote.
  • A lot more attention has been payed towards the first part.
  • He originally said this in conversation with Steve Wozniak at the Hackers Conference in 1984

note:

  • Brand is sometimes criticized for technosolutionism—the believe that for all our problem there will be a technological fix.
  • He himself would most likely just describe himself as a ==technology optimist

Free as in Freedom

  • Freedom 0: the freedom to use the work
  • Freedom 1: the freedom to study the work
  • Freedom 2: the freedom to copy and share the work with others
  • Freedom 3: the freedom to modify the work, and the freedom to distribute modified and therefore derivative works

note:

  • Free also doesn't mean free as in free beer but free as in freedom.
  • Those are the four freedoms that Stallman defined.

note:

  • This is the logo for a can of Free Beer.
  • Free Beer is a brand collaboration between students of IT University of Copenhagenand the artist collective Superflex initiated in 2004.
  • The recipe of the beer is published under a Creative Commons license, granting others the right to freely use and distribute it.
  • The beer itself is sold, but if you wanted to you could brew it at home.

Missionary Church of Kopimism

note:

  • Then again other people take the idea of free information even further
  • The Missionary Church of Kopimism (in Swedish Missionerande Kopimistsamfundet), is a congregation of file sharers who believe that copying information is a sacred virtue
  • It was founded by Isak Gerson, a 19-year-old philosophy student, and Gustav Nipe in Uppsala, Sweden in the autumn of 2010.
  • On January 5, 2012, Kopimism was accepted by Sweden as a legitimate religion with an estimated 4,000 members.

note:

  • The impact of Brand's and Stallman's thinking of free information and software is pretty hard to deny.
  • And without it we probably wouldn't have things like Google, GitHub, Linux, Wikipedia, Blender, Processing, OpenStreetMap, or even things like OpenAI or any other form of artificial intelligence at all
    • all of these projects either rely on free information and/or make information freely available
  • And of course when everything it is free it's hard to earn money with it.
  • Which is maybe why we now have to deal with Ad's and mass surveillance

WikiLeaks

Aaron Swartz, **The Internet's Own Boy

  • He was involved in the development of RSS and Creative Commons, and Markdown
  • And he was a strong advocate for free access to information online

Free Information Part 2: Cats, Pirates, Vikings
...

note:

  • now let's look at a couple examples of information that has been free and some of the issues regarding copy-right

Napster

note:

  • Napster, founded by Shawn Fanning and Sean Parker in 1999, introduced peer-to-peer music sharing.
  • It became very popular, challenging copyright laws and the music industry's business models.
  • Faced legal battles, leading to its shutdown in 2001.
  • Paved the way for modern streaming services and influenced discussions on digital rights and information access.
  • Again, there is a pretty nice documentary of Napster

Piratebay

note:

  • The Pirate Bay (sometimes abbreviated as TPB) is an online index of digital content of entertainment media and software.
  • Founded in 2003 by Swedish think tank Piratbyrån, The Pirate Bay allows visitors to search, download, and contribute magnet links and torrent files, which facilitate peer-to-peer, file sharing among users of the BitTorrent protocol.
  • of course this annoyed a lot of people who want to sell music and movies
  • Yet again, there is a great documentaty on the whole controversy that got sparked around file-sharing TPB AFK

The Techno Viking

note:

  • Techno Viking was one of the early meme’s that went viral
  • It was made by the German experimental video artist Matthias Fritsch
  • it sparked a whole fan culture around it and an exhibiton and online archive
  • but it’s also one of the early cases of infringement of personality rights
  • Because the Techno Viking himself was pretty unhappy with his face traveling across the globe
  • Fritsch was ultimately ordered to pay €13,000 in damages, roughly the same amount he made from YouTube ads and merch
  • He—of course—also made a crowdsourced documentary about it: The Story of the Techno Viking

People Staring at Computers

note:

  • but even within the arts you can get sewed and prosecuted for doing certain things with publicly available data
  • In 2011 For his project People Staring at Computers the artist Kyle McDonald published a series of photos taken with the laptops in two New York City Apple Stores
  • Part of his artistic practice is an ongoing exploration of surveillance, face analysis, and computer-mediated interaction
  • In response, Apple contacted the Secret Service and they raided my apartment.

Scrapism Manifesto, Sam Lavigne
...

  • Someone else who often works with public data is the artist Sam Lavigne
  • Sam Lavigne, outlines a philosophy and practice of using web scraping as an artistic and critical tool
  • He advocates for scraping as a method to analyze and reveal power structures in digital and physical worlds
  • But he emphasizes ethical considerations and the potential of scrapism for activism and social commentary
  • Calls for a reevaluation of data collection, privacy, and the narratives constructed by mass media
